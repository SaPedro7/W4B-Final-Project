Where They're From


Origin of the potato or 'Chunu' The potato originated from southern Peru and was much smaller back then. The first potatoes were grown high in the Andes (South America) from the second century of our era by the Incas. High in the mountains no other plants could grow except for chunu (the potato), so it became popular. The various types of chunu's were eaten fresh as staple food by the Incas, but also dried to flour and even processed into beer. All these chunu's, or 'early potatoes', are derived from one plant from the south of Peru, as shown later by a DNA test. Potato recognized as food Yet it took a long time before the potato in was recognized as food in Europe. In the beginning, the potato was mainly planted in the botanical gardens just for it's looks. The stems and berries of the potato plant are poisonous and therefore the farmers in Europe thought that the tubers were unhealthy as well. At that time potatoes were mainly used as food for pigs or very poor people. In 1727 the potato had it's big moment. It was finally accepted as food in the Netherlands. Slowly but surely the potato gained popularity and got very popular. From the 18th century, the potato was grown in every European country and has since become more and more popular..

Importance of Cleaning


Why It's Important to Wash Potatoes Before Cooking This is why it is extremely important to wash your potatoes before cooking, as anything from bacteria, chemicals, insects and pests could be on the skin of the potato. Bacteria Bacteria is all around us, however, because potatoes are grown outside, they are exposed to a lot of harmful bacteria. Bacteria could find its way onto the potato from farm animal fecal matter, fertilizer and bacteria in the soil which the tuber grow. Chemicals Chemicals such as pesticides and some fertilizer are often used on farms and these can sometimes be found on potatoes. Cleaning Your Potatoes Before Cooking How to Wash Potatoes Properly: A Step by Step Guide Step 1: Use a Vegetable Brush The first step to cleaning your potatoes is to get a vegetable brush and gently scrub on the skin, getting into the eyes to dislodge any dirt that may have found its way on to the potato. Step 2: Soak in a Vinegar Bath Vinegar is really good at killing bacteria and soaking the potatoes is a good way to get rid of the bacteria that has attached itself to the skin of the potato (use 2-3 tablespoons of vinegar in a bowl of warm water). Step 3: Wash the Potato You can now place the potato under the tap and wash off your vegetable wash. Using your hand, swab along the skin of the potato. Step 4: Dry the Potato Once finished, you should dry your potato with paper/kitchen towels before you peel it or throw it in the oven.

Cutting, ow ow


Potatoes Pealer, A Sharp Knife, Cutting Board, Tissue Paper, Water, Crinkle Knife Method First thing first, peal the potatoes; Always have firm grip of potatoes and the pealer; Once pealed, immerse the potatoes in water. Batons/French Fries Have firm grip and hold the potatoes horizontally; Slice the base and place the potato over it; Cut them at least a centimeter thick slice; Place the slices one over the other and cut them slowly having a firm grip over them; Cut the bases in the same way. Wedges Hold the potato side ways and run a knife at the center; Cut it again right at the center twice so as to get 8 wedges out of a potato. Slices Rest the potato horizontally on the cutting board; As per the demand of the recipe, slice the potato having a firm grip over it. Small Dices Rest the potato on the board and cut it in to batons; Run a knife vertically over the batons so as to get small dices of the potato. Medium Dices Cut the potato in to thick strips; Run a knife over them so as to get medium dices. Large Dices Slices away all the edges and cut the potato thickly so as to get large dices. Crinkle Cut Hold the Crinkle knife at 90 degree at the top of the potato and push it downwards; Lay these slices and push the knife downwards.
